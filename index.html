1. Real-Time Sleep Stage Detection Algorithm Development (2023-2024)

Nightly sleep patterns play an important role in clinically evaluating sleep quality. These patterns are commonly characterised by distinct sleep stages quantifiable through EEG measurements. Stage stages (Awake, N1, N2, N3, REM), traditionally, are classified by sleep technicians throughout all-night EEG recordings. While this method is accurate, it is, however, time-consuming. In response, the project aims to develop an AI-based sleep stage classifier capable of automating and expediting this classification process. The algorithm development comprises the process of artefact removal, feature extraction and ML algorithm development.

Artefact Removal

EEG signals, when recorded, are susceptable to contamination from artefacts. These artefacts are not inherent to the neural activity being measured but are introduced during the recording process. It is crucial to remove artefacts as contaminated EEG data can profoundly affect the analysis. Previous studies have employed various techniques to tackle this issue, including Regression, ICA, BSS, and Wavelet Transform.

In this project, our methodology is
○ Notch filters for specific frequncy removal
○ Implemented regression model to remove EOG disturbance
○ Applied ICA to isolate significant signals from unwanted noise
○ Developed an algorithm to select meaningful signals from independent components

Feature Extraction

The collected data are segmented into 30-second EEG signal segments. From each segment, time domain and frequency domain features are extracted. These features serve as inputs for ML algorithms.

ML Algorithm Development

With the extracted features, the ML model is trained to classify sleep stages.



2. Particle Moving Algorithm Development (2022)

Github

Battery research has become of critical significance due to its important role in addressing energy and sustainability challenges. The increasing demand for portable electronics, electric vehicles and renewable energy storage solutions highlights the importance of developing efficient, durable and cost-effective batteries.
Our research team focused on the field of computational modelling to improve battery performance. In our project, they used ABAQUS, an engineering software, to simulate the mechanical systems of cathodes in batteries. To visualise these systems, they needed to generate active particles (AP). I specifically contributed to automating the creation of APs in ABAQUS for a high-volume fraction polymer matrix in lithium-ion batteries. The involved developing Python scripts to algorithmically generate coordinates for the APs, which significantly streamlined the simulation process.

Methods


1. N particles are generated inside a box (initial configuration). Some of these particles might overlap so it is required to reposition.
2. The overlapping particles are identified and an "overlap function f(x)" is calculated, which takes the form "f(x) = a + b", where "a" and "b" are individual overlaps between particles.
3. Selecting one of these overlapping particles at random and moving it to a new position within the box. This is done to try and reduce the overlap between particles.
4. Evaluating the move. If the move reduces the overall overlap, it is accepted; otherwise, the move is rejected and the particle is returned to its original position.
5. The process of moving particles is repeated until the overlap function f(x) reaches zero, meaning no particles overlap.
The process is typical of iterative algorithms used in statistical mechanics and computational materials science to find the equilibrium states of systems.


Results


Initial configuration: spheres are clustered and arranged in a disordered manner.
Final configuration: the spheres are not overlapping with each other and have settled into a more ordered arrangement.

1. Monte Carlo Simulation for Compton Scattering(2023)

Github

Compton scattering is a phenomenon that occurs when a photon interacts with an outer orbital electron. This interaction results in the transfer of energy from the photon to the electron, causing the photon to be deflected from its original path. To investigate this process, our project involved both practical experiments and computational simulations.
During our practical experiment, we directed photons emitted by Cs-137 and meticulously measured the energies of the scattered photons across different angles. Meanwhile, in our computational simulations, we developed a program that generates random events with statistical distributions for photon positions, directions and angles to simulate this phenomenon in a 2D environment. By comparing our experimental findings with computational simulations, we could obtain a comprehensive understanding of Compton Scattering.

Example

The plot shows random paths of photons in one simulation. The rod represents an object that photons are interacting with. The blue lines are the paths that photons take within the simulation.



2. Angular Analysis of the Decay \(B^{0}\rightarrow K^{*0}\mu^{+}\mu^{-}\)(2022)

Github

The Standard Model (SM) is a framework to understand the behaviour of fundamental particles and their interactions through EM, weak and strong forces. In particle physics research, scientists test the predictions of the SM against experimental observations to ensure its consistency. Any deviations from expected patterns in these observations may indicate the presence of phenomena beyond the SM.
In this project, our primary focus was on investigating B meson decay using a large dataset collected from LHcb. Our goal was to analyse the coefficients of a specific angular distribution equation to uncover any deviations from the expected patterns predicted by the Standard Model. Our analysis included several steps: removing background noise from the signal, applying acceptance functions, fitting the data with the angular distribution equation, quantifying errors and ultimately comparing the results with those predicted by the Standard Model.

Angular Distribution Equation

The angular distribution of B meson decay that we investigated. We analysed the coefficients to test theoretical predictions.

*These coefficients are also known as observables, referring to measurable quantities that can be observed or detected in fundamental particle experiments.

4. Data Analysis for discovering the Higgs Boson (2022)

Higgs boson is one of the fundamental particles associated with the quantum excitation of the Higgs field. The Higgs boson was not discovered until the ATLAS and CMS experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) in 2012.
Physicists are interested in the regions where the signal is more significant than merely the background. However, sometimes they might unintentionally optimise the analysis in certain regions that have a higher chance of rare decays happening to obtain expected results. To avoid such bias, blind analysis is presented. The regions that have more decay events than expected are blinded during the process of data analysis, and only the region satisfied with the criteria can be unblinded.
The project performed a simplified version of data analysis for discovering the Higgs boson. It aims to optimise the analysis in the region that has the highest chance of detecting the signal, where the region is selected by blind analysis.


Programming Tool Creation

We developed programming tools using numerical methods for solving integration and optimisation problems in this project. These tools include

Integration:
○ Trapezoidal Rule
○ Simpson's Rule
○ Monte Carlo Methods
○ ODE Methods

Optimisation:
○ Univariant Methods
○ Gradient Descent


Blind Analysis

We pinpointed the region where Higgs decay events showed a significant surplus over background noise and quantified this excess using signal significance (S). Through thorough data analysis, our goal was to differentiate genuine Higgs decay occurrences from background events and noise.
